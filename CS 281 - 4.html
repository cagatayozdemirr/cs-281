Who Uses Databases & DBA Roles Slides 14–15

The slides identify three groups who use databases and describe the Database Administrator (DBA) role specifically. This was missing from the original guide.

  • End users and DBMS vendors: People who query data and the companies that build and sell database systems (Oracle, IBM, Microsoft).
  • DB application programmers: Developers who build applications on top of a DBMS, e.g., web developers building a student portal that queries the university database.
  • Database Administrator (DBA): The most technically demanding role. The DBA's specific responsibilities from the slides:
    • Designs logical and physical schemas
    • Handles security and authorization (who can see what)
    • Ensures data availability and manages crash recovery
    • Database tuning as needs evolve (adding indexes, restructuring)

The slides note: "Must understand how a DBMS works!" — the DBA is not just an administrator, they are a technical expert who must understand the internals of the system they manage.

Why Study Databases? Slide 5

The slides give three reasons this field is important:

  • Shift from computation to information: Computing used to be primarily about number-crunching. Now it is about storing, retrieving, and making sense of enormous information collections — the web, social media, scientific data.
  • Datasets are exploding in diversity and volume: Digital libraries, interactive video, the Human Genome Project — all require database technology to be useful at scale.
  • DBMS encompasses most of CS: Operating systems (for disk management), programming languages (for query languages), theory (for optimisation proofs), AI (for query optimisation), multimedia, logic — all of computer science touches database systems.

π_sname(σ_rating>8(S2))

◆ Ex 7 — Project multiple columns, verify no duplicates
π_rating,age(S2)

Pairs from S2: (9,35.0), (8,55.5), (5,35.0), (10,35.0). All four pairs are distinct (even though age=35.0 repeats, the rating values differ). Result: all 4 rows. Duplicate elimination only removes rows that are COMPLETELY identical in ALL projected columns.

🔴 Tricky Ex 8 — What if projected columns have all-same values?
π_rating(σ_age=35.0(S2))

First σ_age=35.0: keeps yuppy (9,35), guppy (5,35), rusty (10,35). Then π_rating: ratings are 9, 5, 10 — all different. Result: {5, 9, 10}. Three rows. Now try: π_age(σ_age=35.0(S2)). Result has just one row: {35.0}. Even though 3 rows matched, projecting to age gives one unique value.

◆ Ex 9 — Projection is not selection!
π_sname,sid(S1) vs σ_sid=22(S1)

π_sname,sid(S1) keeps two columns, all three rows: (22,dustin),(31,lubber),(58,rusty). σ_sid=22(S1) keeps all four columns but only one row: (22,dustin,7,45.0). Projection = column filter. Selection = row filter. They compose: π_sname(σ_sid=22(S1)) = {dustin}.

◆ Ex 10 — Project a single-column primary key
π_sid(S1)

Since sid is the primary key (unique per row), π_sid(S1) always returns exactly as many rows as S1 has — no deduplication ever occurs when projecting a key. Result: {22, 31, 58}.

σ_rating>8 AND age<40(S2)

Must satisfy BOTH: rating>8 AND age<40. Check: yuppy(9,35)✓✓, lubber(8,55.5)✗(rating not >8), guppy(5,35)✗, rusty(10,35)✓✓. Result: yuppy and rusty.

◆ Ex 4 — Select with OR
σ_rating=10 OR age>50(S2)

Satisfies rating=10 OR age>50: yuppy(9,35)✗✗, lubber(8,55.5)✓(age>50), guppy(5,35)✗✗, rusty(10,35)✓(rating=10). Result: lubber and rusty.

🔴 Ex 7 — Select that returns empty set
σ_rating>10(S2)

No sailor in S2 has rating > 10 (max is 10). Result is an empty relation — still has the same schema (sid,sname,rating,age) but zero rows. Empty sets are valid results in relational algebra.

◆ Ex 8 — Composed selection: narrow step by step
σ_age<40(σ_rating>7(S2))

Inner σ_rating>7: yuppy(9,35), rusty(10,35) [lubber has rating 8 which is not >7, so excluded]. Outer σ_age<40: both yuppy(35) and rusty(35) pass. Result: yuppy and rusty. Note: equivalent to σ_rating>7 AND age<40(S2) — nested selections equal AND conditions.

◆ Ex 9 — Selection then projection vs. projection then selection
π_sname(σ_rating=10(S2)) vs σ_rating=10(π_sname(S2))

First: σ gives rusty(10,35), then π_sname gives {rusty}. Correct. Second: π_sname(S2) gives {yuppy,lubber,guppy,rusty} — but now rating column is GONE. You cannot apply σ_rating=10 anymore! Always apply σ BEFORE π when you need the condition column. Or keep the column in π.

◆ Ex 10 — Select with NOT
σ_NOT(rating=5)(S2)

Equivalent to σ_rating≠5(S2). Removes guppy (rating=5). Result: yuppy, lubber, rusty. The NOT operator inverts the condition.

🔴 Ex 11 — Two-step filter vs. one-step
σ_rating>7(σ_age≥35(S2))

Step 1 σ_age≥35: yuppy(35)✓, lubber(55.5)✓, guppy(35)✓, rusty(35)✓ — all 4 pass! Step 2 σ_rating>7 on all 4: yuppy(9)✓, lubber(8)✓, guppy(5)✗, rusty(10)✓. Result: yuppy, lubber, rusty. Equivalent to σ_rating>7 AND age≥35(S2) — same result, same cost either way.

Union (∪), Intersection (∩), Set-Difference (−) Slide 9

🔑
Union-compatibility requirement

For ∪, ∩, −: both input relations must have the SAME NUMBER of columns AND corresponding columns must have the SAME TYPE. The result schema matches the inputs (by convention, names taken from left operand).

★ Slide Ex 3 — S1 ∩ S2
S1 ∩ S2

sidsnameratingage

◆ Ex 4 — S2 − S1 (opposite direction)
S2 − S1

Rows in S2 NOT in S1. S2 sids not in S1: 28(yuppy), 44(guppy). Result: yuppy and guppy. Note: S1−S2 ≠ S2−S1. Set-difference is NOT commutative!

◆ Ex 5 — Union with selection
σ_rating=10(S1) ∪ σ_rating=10(S2)

σ_rating=10(S1): rusty(58,10,35). σ_rating=10(S2): rusty(58,10,35). Union: just rusty (same row, one copy). When a sailor appears in both S1 and S2 with rating 10, union still includes them once.

◆ Ex 6 — Intersection using set-difference
R1 ∩ R2 = R1 − (R1 − R2)

Intersection is not a primitive operator. You can express A∩B as A−(A−B). "Remove from A everything that's not in B — what's left is in both." Useful to remember for exams that test derivation of operators.

◆ Ex 7 — Union then project
π_sname(S1 ∪ S2)

S1∪S2 = 5 distinct sailors (dustin,yuppy,lubber,guppy,rusty). Then π_sname: {dustin, yuppy, lubber, guppy, rusty}. Same as π_sname(S1) ∪ π_sname(S2) in this case — they commute when projecting a key that guarantees uniqueness.

🔴 Ex 8 — Non-union-compatible: this FAILS
S1 ∪ R1 ← ILLEGAL

S1 has schema (sid,sname,rating,age) — 4 columns. R1 has schema (sid,bid,day) — 3 columns. Different number of columns → NOT union-compatible → this operation is undefined and illegal. Always check compatibility before applying set operations.

◆ Ex 9 — π makes schemas compatible
π_sid(S1) ∪ π_sid(R1)

π_sid(S1) = {22,31,58}. π_sid(R1) = {22,58}. Both are now (sid:integer) — union-compatible. Union = {22,31,58}. This is how you union relations with different schemas: project to the common attribute first.

◆ Ex 10 — What does S1 ∩ S2 tell you semantically?

S1 ∩ S2 gives sailors who appear in BOTH S1 AND S2 with IDENTICAL attribute values. If lubber in S1 had rating=7 but lubber in S2 had rating=8, they'd be different tuples — intersection would exclude both. Intersection requires complete row equality, not just same primary key.

Cross-Product and Renaming ρ Slide 10 ★ Renaming was missing from Guide 1

The renaming operator ρ was completely absent from the original guide. It is essential for resolving naming conflicts when the same attribute name appears in both relations of a cross-product.

Syntax: ρ(C(1→sid1, 5→sid2), S1 × R1) means: take S1 × R1, and rename field 1 to "sid1" and field 5 to "sid2". Field numbers are positional (field 1 is the leftmost).

Examples
★ Slide Ex 1 — S1 × R1 with naming conflict
S1 × R1

S1 has (sid,sname,rating,age). R1 has (sid,bid,day). Both have a column called "sid" — conflict! The result has 6 columns but two named "sid". This is a problem. The cross-product produces all 3×2=6 rows:

22dustin745.02210110/10/96
22dustin745.05810311/12/96
31lubber855.52210110/10/96
31lubber855.55810311/12/96
58rusty1035.02210110/10/96
58rusty1035.05810311/12/96
★ Slide Ex 2 — Renaming to resolve conflict
ρ(C(1→sid1, 5→sid2), S1 × R1)

Fields of S1×R1 in order: field1=S1.sid, field2=sname, field3=rating, field4=age, field5=R1.sid, field6=bid, field7=day. Rename field 1 to sid1, field 5 to sid2. Now the schema is (sid1, sname, rating, age, sid2, bid, day) — no name conflict. We can now refer unambiguously to each column.

◆ Ex 3 — Why cross-product alone is rarely useful
S1 × R1 produces nonsensical pairs:

Row (dustin,22,7,45.0,58,103,11/12/96): dustin paired with rusty's reservation. This is meaningless. Cross-product pairs EVERY sailor with EVERY reservation. You need a join condition to pick out only the meaningful combinations (where sailor.sid = reservation.sid).

◆ Ex 4 — Self-join needs renaming
ρ(S(1→sid1, 2→sname1), S1) × ρ(S(1→sid2, 2→sname2), S1)

To join Sailors with itself (e.g., "find pairs of sailors with same rating"), you must first rename both copies so they have different column names. Without renaming, both copies have the same schema and you can't distinguish them.

◆ Ex 5 — Counting cross-product size

|S1 × R1| = |S1| × |R1| = 3 × 2 = 6 rows. |S2 × R1| = 4 × 2 = 8 rows. |S1 × S2| = 3 × 4 = 12 rows. The cross-product grows exponentially — a key reason the query optimiser never literally computes cross-products but uses join algorithms instead.

◆ Ex 6 — Number of columns in cross-product

S1 has 4 columns, R1 has 3 columns. S1 × R1 has 4+3=7 columns. S1 has 4, Boats B has 3 (bid,bname,color). S1 × B has 4+3=7 columns. Cross-product columns always ADD.

Condition Join (θ-join), Equi-Join, Natural Join Slides 11–12

A condition join R ⋈c S = σc(R × S). An equi-join is a condition join where all conditions are equalities. A natural join is an equi-join on ALL common column names, with duplicate columns removed.

★ Slide Ex 3 — Natural join Sailors ⋈ Reserves
S1 ⋈ R1 (natural join — automatically joins on common attribute "sid")

Same result as the equi-join above — natural join automatically uses all common attribute names. "sid" is the only common column. Result: same 2 rows. Natural join is the most concise notation.

◆ Ex 4 — Three-way natural join
Sailors ⋈ Reserves ⋈ Boats

Sailors and Reserves share "sid". Reserves and Boats share "bid". A three-way natural join produces rows where sailor.sid = reserve.sid AND reserve.bid = boat.bid. Result: rows like (dustin,22,7,45,101,10/10/96,101,Interlake,blue) — dustin reserved blue boat Interlake. This is how most "find info about reservations" queries work.

◆ Ex 5 — Join then select

S1 ⋈ R1 gives (dustin,22,101) and (rusty,58,103). Then σ_bid=103: only rusty's row passes. Result: (rusty,58,10,35,103,11/12/96).

◆ Ex 6 — Select then join (more efficient)
S1 ⋈ σ_bid=103(R1)

σ_bid=103(R1) = {(58,103,11/12/96)}. Then join with S1 on sid: S1 sailor with sid=58 is rusty. Same result as Ex 5 but more efficient — filter before joining reduces the size of the join input. The query optimiser does exactly this.

◆ Ex 7 — Condition join with inequality
S1 ⋈_{rating > 7} R1 (cross-product where sailor rating > 7)

This is NOT a natural join — it's a condition join with a non-equality. All S1×R1 combinations where S1.rating>7. Dustin(7) has rating=7, not >7. Lubber(8)>7 ✓, rusty(10)>7 ✓. Each pairs with both R1 rows: 4 result rows. (The sailor column sid and reservation column sid are both in the result, renamed.)

◆ Ex 8 — Natural join with no common columns
Sailors ⋈ Boats (no common columns!)

Sailors(sid,sname,rating,age) and Boats(bid,bname,color) share NO column names. Natural join on no columns = cross-product. |Sailors|×|Boats|= 3×4=12 rows. Every sailor paired with every boat. Use with caution — always verify common columns exist before using natural join.

🔴 Ex 9 — Join vs. cross-product size comparison

S1 (3 rows) × R1 (2 rows) = 6 rows. S1 ⋈ R1 = 2 rows. Join is much smaller because most sailor/reservation combinations don't share the same sid. The join condition "sid=sid" acts as a very selective filter. In practice, joins are orders of magnitude smaller than cross-products on the same data.

◆ Ex 10 — Self-join to find sailors in same rating group
ρ(S(1→sid1,2→sname1,3→r1), S2) ⋈_{r1=rating AND sid1<sid2} S2

Join S2 with itself where ratings match AND sid1<sid2 (to avoid duplicate pairs). Finds pairs of different sailors with the same rating. In S2: yuppy(9) and lubber(8) and guppy(5) and rusty(10). No two have the same rating, so result is empty.

◆ Ex 11 — Join used to "look up" information
π_sname,bname(Sailors ⋈ Reserves ⋈ Boats)

Join all three tables, then project to just sailor name and boat name. Result: (dustin, Interlake) — dustin reserved the Interlake. (rusty, Clipper) — rusty reserved the Clipper. This is the most common pattern in SQL queries: join multiple tables, then project to needed columns.

Division ÷ — The "For All" Operator

10+ examples with the slide's A/B1/B2/B3 table

Division A ÷ B Slides 13–15

A ÷ B returns all x values from A such that for EVERY y value in B, the pair (x,y) exists in A. Used for "find all X that relate to every Y" queries.

Examples
★ Slide Ex 1 — A/B1, A/B2, A/B3 (the exact slide example)

Relation A(sno,pno): (s1,p1),(s1,p2),(s1,p3),(s1,p4),(s2,p1),(s2,p2),(s3,p2),(s4,p2),(s4,p4)

B1(pno): {p2} — A÷B1: which sno values appear with p2? s1(p2)✓, s2(p2)✓, s3(p2)✓, s4(p2)✓. Result: {s1,s2,s3,s4} — all of them.

B2(pno): {p2,p4} — A÷B2: which sno has BOTH p2 AND p4? s1(p2,p4)✓, s4(p2,p4)✓. s2 has p1,p2 — missing p4✗. s3 has only p2 — missing p4✗. Result: {s1,s4}

B3(pno): {p1,p2,p4} — A÷B3: which sno has ALL of p1,p2,p4? s1(p1✓,p2✓,p4✓)✓. s2(p1✓,p2✓,p4?)✗ — s2 missing p4. s3,s4 missing p1. Result: {s1}

★ Slide Ex 2 — Find sailors who've reserved ALL boats
(Tempsids, π_sid,bid(Reserves) ÷ π_bid(Boats)) sname(Tempsids ⋈ Sailors)

Step 1: π_sid,bid(Reserves) = all (sailor,boat) pairs ever reserved. Step 2: π_bid(Boats) = all boat IDs that exist. Step 3: Division gives sids of sailors who appear with EVERY boat. Step 4: Join with Sailors to get names.

★ Slide Ex 3 — Find sailors who've reserved all 'Interlake' boats
π_sid,bid(Reserves) ÷ π_bid(σ_bname='Interlake'(Boats))

Right side: select only Interlake boats from Boats, then project to bid. Division: which sailors have reserved EVERY Interlake boat? Sailors must appear with every bid from the Interlake subset.

◆ Ex 4 — Division with single element in divisor = selection

If B has just one row (one value), then A÷B = all x values from A that appear with that one y value. This is equivalent to π_x(σ_y=v(A)) for the single value v in B. Division with a singleton divisor degenerates to a selection+projection.

◆ Ex 5 — Expressing division using basic operators
A ÷ B = π_x(A) − π_x((π_x(A) × B) − A)

Step 1: π_x(A) = all x values in A. Step 2: π_x(A) × B = all possible (x,y) combinations that COULD be in A. Step 3: (π_x(A)×B) − A = combinations that SHOULD be there but AREN'T. Step 4: π_x(...) = x values that are missing some y. Step 5: π_x(A) minus those = x values that are missing NOTHING = have all y values.

◆ Ex 6 — Apply the formula to A/B2
A ÷ B2 where B2 = {p2, p4}

π_sno(A) = {s1,s2,s3,s4}. π_sno(A)×B2 = {(s1,p2),(s1,p4),(s2,p2),(s2,p4),(s3,p2),(s3,p4),(s4,p2),(s4,p4)} — all combinations. (π_sno(A)×B2)−A: which of these don't exist in A? (s2,p4) — s2 never reserved p4. (s3,p4) — s3 never reserved p4. π_sno of those: {s2,s3}. A÷B2 = {s1,s2,s3,s4}−{s2,s3} = {s1,s4}. Matches!

◆ Ex 7 — Dividing with empty divisor

If B is empty (no rows), then A÷B = π_x(A) — ALL x values. Because the condition "for every y in B, (x,y) is in A" is vacuously true when B is empty. Every sailor trivially "reserved every boat" when there are no boats to reserve. Know this edge case for exams.

🔴 Ex 8 — Divisor must be a subset of dividend schema

A(sno,pno) ÷ B(pno): works — B.pno appears in A. A(sno,pno) ÷ B(bno): FAILS — bno not in A's schema. Division requires the divisor's attributes to be a subset of the dividend's attributes. Always verify schemas before applying ÷.

Join Reserves and Sailors first (on sid), then filter where bid=103, then project. Same result. The query optimiser would prefer Solution 1 (filter early to reduce join size).

★ Query 2 — Find names of sailors who've reserved a RED boat
Solution 1 (straightforward): π_sname(σ_color='red'(Boats) ⋈ Reserves ⋈ Sailors) Solution 2 (more efficient — push selection down): π_sname(σ_sid=sid AND bid=bid AND color='red'(σ_color='red'(Boats) ⋈ Reserves) ⋈ Sailors)

Solution 1: filter Boats for color='red' (keeps 102,104), join with Reserves (which reservations used a red boat?), join with Sailors (who made those reservations?), project sname. Result: sailors who reserved boat 102 or 104.

The slides say: "A query optimiser can find the efficient solution given the first solution!" — you write it cleanly, the optimiser makes it fast.

★ Query 3 — Find sailors who've reserved a red OR green boat
Tempboats ← σ_color='red' OR color='green'(Boats) π_sname(Tempboats ⋈ Reserves ⋈ Sailors)

Filter boats to red and green ones, then join with reservations and sailors. The slide asks: "Can also define Tempboats using union?" Yes:

Tempboats ← σ_color='red'(Boats) ∪ σ_color='green'(Boats)

The slide also asks: "What if OR is replaced by AND?" — then Tempboats would be boats that are BOTH red AND green (impossible for a single boat). Tempboats would be empty. Result: empty. This is why the next query (red AND green) needs a completely different approach.

★ Query 4 — Find sailors who've reserved a RED AND a GREEN boat
Tempred ← π_sid(σ_color='red'(Boats) ⋈ Reserves) Tempgreen ← π_sid(σ_color='green'(Boats) ⋈ Reserves) π_sname((Tempred ∩ Tempgreen) ⋈ Sailors)

Tempred = sids of sailors who reserved a red boat. Tempgreen = sids who reserved a green boat. Intersection = sids who reserved BOTH. Join with Sailors to get names. The slides note: sid is a key for Sailors, so the intersection on sid is clean.

The approach from Query 3 DOESN'T work here because "reserved a red AND green boat" cannot be expressed as a condition on a single reservation row — you need two separate reservations.

★ Query 5 — Find names of sailors who've reserved ALL boats
Tempsids ← π_sid,bid(Reserves) ÷ π_bid(Boats) π_sname(Tempsids ⋈ Sailors)

π_sid,bid(Reserves): all (sailor,boat) reservation pairs. π_bid(Boats): all boat IDs. Division: sailors who appear with EVERY boat ID. Then join with Sailors for names. Using our data: only a sailor who has reservations for boats 101,102,103,104 would appear.

The slide also gives the variant: "Find sailors who've reserved all Interlake boats" — just change the right side of division to π_bid(σ_bname='Interlake'(Boats)).

◆ Practice — Find ages of sailors who've reserved boat 101
π_age(σ_bid=101(Reserves) ⋈ Sailors)

σ_bid=101(R1) = {(22,101,10/10/96)}. Join with Sailors on sid=22: dustin(45.0). π_age: {45.0}.

◆ Practice — Find sids of sailors who have NOT made any reservation
π_sid(Sailors) − π_sid(Reserves)

π_sid(Sailors) = {22,31,58} (from S1). π_sid(Reserves) = {22,58}. Difference: {31} — lubber has not reserved any boat.

🔴 Practice — Find sailors who've reserved ONLY red boats (every reservation was red)
Step 1: find sailors who've reserved at least one non-red boat: NonRed ← π_sid(Reserves ⋈ σ_color≠'red'(Boats)) Step 2: remove them from all sailors who've made reservations: π_sname((π_sid(Reserves) − NonRed) ⋈ Sailors)

This is the "set-difference trick" for "only" queries. Find who did the thing you DON'T want, then subtract them from the total. Very common exam pattern.

Conceptual Evaluation and Basic Queries Slides 3–8

SQL evaluates conceptually as: cross-product → filter with WHERE → project with SELECT → eliminate duplicates with DISTINCT. The optimiser finds a faster actual execution, but the result is identical.

Examples — write out the full query, trace it through
★ Slide Ex 1 — Exact conceptual evaluation example from slide 5
SELECT S.sname FROM Sailors S, Reserves R WHERE S.sid = R.sid AND R.bid = 103

Step 1: cross-product S×R = 6 rows (3 sailors × 2 reservations). Step 2: apply WHERE. sid=sid AND bid=103. Only (rusty,58)×(58,103) satisfies. Step 3: project sname. Result: {rusty}. The slides show the full cross-product table — this is exactly what the optimiser avoids computing in practice.

★ Slide Ex 2 — Reserve at least one boat (with DISTINCT discussion)
SELECT S.sid FROM Sailors S, Reserves R WHERE S.sid = R.sid

Returns sids of sailors who have reserved at least one boat. The slide asks: "Would DISTINCT make a difference?" YES — if a sailor reserved multiple boats, their sid appears multiple times without DISTINCT. Adding DISTINCT: SELECT DISTINCT S.sid removes duplicate sids.

"What if we replace S.sid by S.sname?" Two sailors could have the same name — different sids but same name. DISTINCT would then matter for names too, though for different reasons.

★ Slide Ex 3 — String matching with LIKE and computed columns
SELECT S.age, age1=S.age-5, 2*S.age AS age2 FROM Sailors S WHERE S.sname LIKE 'B_%B'

Pattern 'B_%B': starts with B, then at least one character (_), then zero or more characters (%), then ends with B. Minimum length: 3 ('BAB'). Computed columns: age-5 (named age1 using =), 2*age (named age2 using AS). The two naming styles (= and AS) both work in SQL.

◆ Ex 4 — Range selection
SELECT S.sname, S.rating FROM Sailors S WHERE S.rating BETWEEN 7 AND 9

Equivalent to WHERE rating >= 7 AND rating <= 9. From S2: yuppy(9)✓, lubber(8)✓, guppy(5)✗, rusty(10)✗. Result: yuppy and lubber.

◆ Ex 5 — SELECT * (all columns)
SELECT * FROM Sailors S WHERE S.age < 40

Returns all columns for sailors under 40. From S2: yuppy(35)✓, lubber(55.5)✗, guppy(35)✓, rusty(35)✓. Result: 3 rows with all 4 columns.

◆ Ex 6 — DISTINCT matters here
SELECT S.age FROM Sailors S -- without DISTINCT SELECT DISTINCT S.age FROM Sailors S -- with DISTINCT

Without DISTINCT (using S2): 35.0, 55.5, 35.0, 35.0 — 4 rows. With DISTINCT: 35.0, 55.5 — 2 rows. SQL by default keeps duplicates (unlike relational algebra which always removes them). DISTINCT = explicit deduplication.

◆ Ex 7 — LIKE patterns practice
WHERE sname LIKE '%e%' -- contains 'e' WHERE sname LIKE '_____' -- exactly 5 chars (5 underscores) WHERE sname LIKE 'r%' -- starts with r → 'rusty' WHERE sname LIKE '%y' -- ends with y → 'dusty','yuppy','rusty','guppy'

_ matches EXACTLY one character. % matches ZERO OR MORE characters. Case-sensitivity depends on the database system (MySQL case-insensitive by default, PostgreSQL case-sensitive).

◆ Ex 8 — Range variable always needed for self-join
SELECT S1.sname, S2.sname FROM Sailors S1, Sailors S2 WHERE S1.rating = S2.rating AND S1.sid < S2.sid -- avoid (A,B) and (B,A) duplicates

Find pairs of different sailors with the same rating. Without the range variables S1 and S2, you couldn't write "Sailors.rating = Sailors.rating." Range variables let you reference the same table twice as if they were two different tables.

◆ Ex 9 — Three-table join
SELECT S.sname, B.bname, B.color FROM Sailors S, Reserves R, Boats B WHERE S.sid = R.sid AND R.bid = B.bid

Find what boats each sailor has reserved (with boat name and colour). Conceptually: S×R×B (3×2×4=24 rows), then WHERE eliminates non-matching, giving sailor/reservation/boat triples. Result: (dustin, Interlake, blue), (rusty, Clipper, green).

🔴 Ex 10 — Why ORDER MATTERS in conceptual evaluation
SELECT S.sname FROM Sailors S, Reserves R, Boats B WHERE S.sid = R.sid AND R.bid = B.bid AND B.color = 'red'

Conceptual: cross-product S×R×B = 3×2×4=24 rows. Filter: S.sid=R.sid (keeps 4), R.bid=B.bid (joins reservations to boats), color='red'. Result: (dustin, red boat 102)? Wait — dustin reserved bid=101 (Interlake, BLUE), not red. Rusty reserved bid=103 (Clipper, GREEN), not red. Neither sailor reserved a red boat in our data! Result is empty. Always trace through the actual data.

◆ Ex 11 — Arithmetic in WHERE
SELECT S.sname FROM Sailors S WHERE S.age * 2 > S.rating * 10

Filter where age×2 > rating×10. From S1: dustin(45×2=90 vs 7×10=70 → 90>70)✓. lubber(55.5×2=111 vs 8×10=80 → 111>80)✓. rusty(35×2=70 vs 10×10=100 → 70>100)✗. Result: dustin and lubber.

◆ Ex 3 — UNION ALL (keeping duplicates)
SELECT sid FROM Sailors UNION ALL SELECT sid FROM Reserves

UNION ALL keeps duplicates from both sides. If sid=22 appears in Sailors AND Reserves, it appears twice. Use UNION ALL when you need to count total occurrences or when you know there are no duplicates (faster since no deduplication step).

◆ Ex 4 — EXCEPT (set-difference in SQL)

Sailors who have NOT made any reservation. π_sid(Sailors) − π_sid(Reserves) in relational algebra. Result: lubber(31) — appears in Sailors but not in Reserves (R1).

◆ Ex 5 — Union of different selections on same table

Rating=10: rusty. Age<40 (from extended table): zorba(16), horatio(35), brutus(33), rusty(35). Union: {rusty, zorba, horatio, brutus} — rusty appears in both but once in result.

◆ Ex 6 — Three-way union

Three UNION is fine — evaluated left to right. Sailors rating>9: rusty(58), zorba(71). Age<30 (extended): zorba(16). Reserves bid=101: dustin(22). Union: {58, 71, 22}. Zorba appears in first two but deduplicated.

🔴 Ex 7 — INTERSECT rewritten as IN (important when INTERSECT unavailable)

Chapter 5 · Nested Queries

Nested Queries, EXISTS, ANY, ALL, Division in SQL

10+ examples including both forms of division

Nested Queries — IN, NOT IN, EXISTS, NOT EXISTS, ANY, ALL Slides 11–15

Examples
★ Slide Ex 1 — IN subquery (non-correlated)
SELECT S.sname FROM Sailors S WHERE S.sid IN (SELECT R.sid FROM Reserves R WHERE R.bid = 103)

Subquery runs once: returns {58}. Outer: check each sailor if sid IN {58}. Only rusty(58) passes. Equivalent to the join version but semantically clearer. The subquery is "non-correlated" — it doesn't reference the outer query, so it runs once.

★ Slide Ex 2 — EXISTS (correlated subquery)
SELECT S.sname FROM Sailors S WHERE EXISTS (SELECT * FROM Reserves R WHERE R.bid = 103 AND S.sid = R.sid)

For EACH sailor S, the subquery re-runs checking if that specific sailor reserved boat 103. S.sid references the outer query — this is a "correlated" subquery. EXISTS returns true if the subquery returns at least one row. Same result as IN version: {rusty}. More expensive (subquery runs once per outer row) but more expressive.

★ Slide Ex 3 — UNIQUE operator
SELECT S.sname FROM Sailors S WHERE UNIQUE (SELECT R.bid FROM Reserves R WHERE R.bid = 103 AND S.sid = R.sid)

UNIQUE is true if the subquery returns at most one row (no duplicates). This finds sailors with AT MOST ONE reservation for boat 103. The slide explains why * is replaced by R.bid: UNIQUE checks for duplicate TUPLES. SELECT * would include the day column — two reservations on different days would have different full tuples even if same bid. Using just R.bid correctly checks if the same boat appears twice.

★ Slide Ex 4 — ANY operator
SELECT * FROM Sailors S WHERE S.rating > ANY (SELECT S2.rating FROM Sailors S2 WHERE S2.sname = 'horatio')

Finds sailors whose rating is greater than SOME sailor named horatio. Subquery returns horatio's rating(s) — say {7}. ANY means: is S.rating > 7 for at least one value in {7}? So sailors with rating > 7 qualify. Equivalent to: rating > MIN(ratings of horatio).

★ Slide Ex 5 — Division in SQL: NOT EXISTS ... EXCEPT
-- Find sailors who've reserved ALL boats (version 1) SELECT S.sname FROM Sailors S WHERE NOT EXISTS ( (SELECT B.bid FROM Boats B) -- all boat ids EXCEPT (SELECT R.bid FROM Reserves R -- boats this sailor reserved WHERE R.sid = S.sid) )

"Find sailors where there is NO boat ID that the sailor has NOT reserved." The EXCEPT gives "boats not yet reserved by this sailor." NOT EXISTS of that set = "there are no un-reserved boats" = "all boats are reserved."

★ Slide Ex 6 — Division in SQL: double NOT EXISTS (version 2)
-- Same query, without EXCEPT (works in all SQL systems) SELECT S.sname FROM Sailors S WHERE NOT EXISTS ( SELECT B.bid FROM Boats B WHERE NOT EXISTS ( SELECT R.bid FROM Reserves R WHERE R.bid = B.bid AND R.sid = S.sid ) )

Read it: "Find sailors S such that there is no boat B for which there is no reservation by S for B." Triple negation = "every boat has been reserved by S." The slides label it: "Sailors S such that... there is no boat B without... a Reserves tuple showing S reserved B." This is the universal standard for expressing division in SQL.

◆ Ex 7 — NOT IN (exclude sailors who reserved 103)
SELECT S.sname FROM Sailors S WHERE S.sid NOT IN (SELECT R.sid FROM Reserves R WHERE R.bid = 103)

The slide mentions this: "To find sailors who've NOT reserved #103, use NOT IN." From our data: R.bid=103 gives {58}. NOT IN {58}: dustin(22) and lubber(31) qualify.

◆ Ex 8 — ALL operator
SELECT S.sname FROM Sailors S WHERE S.rating >= ALL (SELECT S2.rating FROM Sailors S2)

Finds sailors whose rating is ≥ ALL other ratings. Equivalent to: rating = MAX(rating). From S1: only rusty(10) has rating ≥ all others. Result: {rusty}. Note: ALL with >: "greater than all" = "greater than maximum." Use ALL for "best/highest/lowest" queries.

🔴 Ex 9 — NOT IN vs NOT EXISTS with NULL (critical difference)
-- If subquery can return NULL, NOT IN behaves unexpectedly! SELECT S.sname FROM Sailors S WHERE S.sid NOT IN (SELECT R.sid FROM Reserves R) -- vs SELECT S.sname FROM Sailors S WHERE NOT EXISTS (SELECT * FROM Reserves R WHERE R.sid=S.sid)

If Reserves.sid can be NULL: NOT IN fails because "x NOT IN {1, 2, NULL}" evaluates to UNKNOWN (three-valued logic). NOT EXISTS handles NULL correctly. In production SQL, prefer NOT EXISTS over NOT IN when NULLs are possible. On exams, the slides don't stress this — but know the difference.

◆ Ex 10 — Nested query in FROM clause
SELECT S.sname FROM (SELECT * FROM Sailors WHERE rating > 7) AS S WHERE S.age < 40

A subquery in the FROM clause creates a "derived table." SQL allows subqueries in SELECT, FROM, and HAVING, not just WHERE. This is equivalent to: SELECT sname FROM Sailors WHERE rating > 7 AND age < 40. But derived tables are essential for aggregation queries (see the GROUP BY section).

★ Slide Ex 1 — All simple aggregates from the slide
SELECT COUNT(*) -- count all rows → 4 (from S2) FROM Sailors S SELECT AVG(S.age) -- average age of rating-10 sailors FROM Sailors S WHERE S.rating = 10 -- rusty(35) → avg = 35.0 SELECT COUNT(DISTINCT S.rating) -- distinct ratings among 'Bob' sailors FROM Sailors S WHERE S.sname = 'Bob' -- → 0 (no Bob in our data) SELECT AVG(DISTINCT S.age) -- average of DISTINCT ages of rating-10 sailors FROM Sailors S WHERE S.rating = 10

DISTINCT in aggregates: COUNT(DISTINCT rating) counts unique rating values. AVG(DISTINCT age) averages unique age values (if two sailors have same age, only counted once). Usually you want AVG without DISTINCT.

★ Slide Ex 2 — Find sailor with highest rating (correct approach)
-- ILLEGAL (slide explicitly marks this wrong): SELECT S.sname, MAX(S.age) ← ILLEGAL FROM Sailors S -- CORRECT — subquery approach: SELECT S.sname, S.age FROM Sailors S WHERE S.age = (SELECT MAX(S2.age) FROM Sailors S2) -- Also correct (SQL/92 — some systems don't support): SELECT S.sname, S.age FROM Sailors S WHERE (SELECT MAX(S2.age) FROM Sailors S2) = S.age

Why is sname+MAX(age) illegal? When you use MAX(age), the result is ONE number. But sname is different for each row — the DBMS doesn't know WHICH sname to show. The rule: any non-aggregate column in SELECT must uniquely take one value per output row. The subquery approach gets around this by finding the max age first, then selecting sailors with that age.

★ Slide Ex 3 — Find sailor with highest rating (using MAX subquery)
SELECT S.sname FROM Sailors S WHERE S.rating = (SELECT MAX(S2.rating) FROM Sailors S2)

From S2: MAX(rating) = 10. WHERE rating=10: rusty(10). Result: {rusty}. If two sailors had the same maximum rating, BOTH would appear — which is correct.

◆ Ex 4 — COUNT(*) vs COUNT(column)
SELECT COUNT(*), COUNT(rating), COUNT(DISTINCT rating) FROM Sailors

COUNT(*): counts all rows including those with NULLs in any column. COUNT(rating): counts rows where rating IS NOT NULL. COUNT(DISTINCT rating): counts unique non-NULL rating values. If one sailor has rating=NULL: COUNT(*) = 4, COUNT(rating) = 3, COUNT(DISTINCT rating) ≤ 3.

◆ Ex 5 — SUM and MIN
SELECT SUM(S.age), MIN(S.age), MAX(S.age) FROM Sailors S WHERE S.rating >= 8

From S2, rating≥8: yuppy(35), lubber(55.5), rusty(35). SUM(age)=125.5. MIN(age)=35. MAX(age)=55.5.

MIN(AVG(...)) is illegal — SQL does not allow nested aggregate functions. The fix: use a subquery or derived table to compute the inner aggregate first, then apply the outer aggregate to that result. Step 1: compute AVG(age) per rating. Step 2: find the MIN of those averages. Two separate queries.

◆ Ex 7 — Aggregate with expression

Aggregate can operate on expressions, not just column names. Each rating is multiplied by 10 first, then summed. From S2: (9×10)+(8×10)+(5×10)+(10×10) = 90+80+50+100 = 320.

🔴 Ex 8 — Why mixing aggregate and non-aggregate without GROUP BY fails
-- These are ALL illegal without GROUP BY: SELECT sname, COUNT(*) FROM Sailors ← ILLEGAL SELECT bid, MAX(sid) FROM Reserves ← ILLEGAL SELECT color, AVG(bid) FROM Boats ← ILLEGAL

Without GROUP BY, an aggregate collapses the entire table to one row. But sname/bid/color have different values for different rows — which value should appear in that one row? Undefined. Either use GROUP BY (one group per sname value) or don't mix aggregates with non-aggregate columns at all.

★ Slide Ex 1 — Motivation: find min age per rating WITHOUT GROUP BY

★ Slide Ex 3 — For each RED boat, count reservations (join then group)
SELECT B.bid, COUNT(*) AS scount FROM Sailors S, Boats B, Reserves R WHERE S.sid=R.sid AND R.bid=B.bid AND B.color='red' GROUP BY B.bid

Join all three tables (only red boats pass the WHERE filter), then GROUP BY boat id, count reservations per boat. The slide asks: "What if we move B.color='red' from WHERE to HAVING?" → Groups ALL boats, then counts, then HAVING filters to red boats. Different intermediate state but same result. "What if we drop Sailors and the S.sid condition?" → We count distinct reservations (R rows) per boat, not distinct sailors per boat.

★ Slide Ex 4 — HAVING with correlated subquery (most complex GROUP BY example)
SELECT S.rating, MIN(S.age) FROM Sailors S WHERE S.age > 18 GROUP BY S.rating HAVING 1 < (SELECT COUNT(*) FROM Sailors S2 WHERE S.rating = S2.rating)

The HAVING subquery is correlated — it references S.rating from the outer GROUP BY. It counts ALL sailors at this rating level (not just those >18). The slide says: "Compare this with the query where we considered only ratings with 2 sailors over 18!" In Ex 2, HAVING COUNT(*)>1 counts sailors over 18. Here, the subquery counts ALL sailors at that rating (any age). Different semantics.

The slide asks: "What if HAVING COUNT(*)>1 replaces the subquery?" Then it counts sailors over 18 per group (simpler). In both cases, rating=7 has ≥2 sailors with age>18 (dustin, horatio), so rating 7 still qualifies.

◆ Ex 5 — COUNT DISTINCT in GROUP BY
SELECT B.color, COUNT(DISTINCT R.sid) AS unique_sailors FROM Boats B, Reserves R WHERE R.bid = B.bid GROUP BY B.color

Count distinct sailors who reserved each colour of boat. Groups by color, within each group counts unique sids. If dustin reserved two blue boats, he counts once. Result: blue → 1 (dustin), green → 1 (rusty), red → 0 (no one reserved red in our data).

◆ Ex 6 — Multiple aggregates per group
SELECT S.rating, COUNT(*) AS num_sailors, MIN(S.age) AS youngest, MAX(S.age) AS oldest, AVG(S.age) AS avg_age FROM Sailors S GROUP BY S.rating

Multiple aggregates per group — perfectly legal. For rating=7 (dustin+horatio from extended table): count=2, min=35, max=45, avg=40. For rating=10 (zorba+rusty): count=2, min=16, max=35, avg=25.5. Each aggregate is computed independently per group.

◆ Ex 7 — HAVING without aggregate (unusual but legal)
SELECT S.rating, MIN(S.age) FROM Sailors S GROUP BY S.rating HAVING S.rating > 7

HAVING S.rating>7 has no aggregate — but S.rating appears in GROUP BY, so it has one value per group. Legal. This is equivalent to using WHERE S.rating>7 (filter before grouping). WHERE is preferred here (more efficient — filters early). Use HAVING only when the condition NEEDS aggregate values.

🔴 Ex 8 — WHERE vs HAVING: the critical difference
-- Different queries! -- Query A: filter rows BEFORE grouping SELECT rating, COUNT(*) FROM Sailors WHERE age > 30 GROUP BY rating -- Query B: filter groups AFTER grouping SELECT rating, COUNT(*) FROM Sailors GROUP BY rating HAVING COUNT(*) > 1

Query A: only sailors over 30 are put into groups, then counted. zorba(10,16) is excluded before grouping. Query B: all sailors grouped, then groups with only 1 member removed. These are completely different filters. WHERE filters ROWS (before grouping). HAVING filters GROUPS (after grouping). An aggregate like COUNT(*) can ONLY go in HAVING, never in WHERE.

🔴 Ex 9 — GROUP BY column must be in SELECT (or wrapped in aggregate)
-- ILLEGAL: sname not in GROUP BY and not in aggregate SELECT S.rating, S.sname, COUNT(*) ← sname: ILLEGAL FROM Sailors S GROUP BY S.rating -- LEGAL: add sname to GROUP BY SELECT S.rating, S.sname, COUNT(*) FROM Sailors S GROUP BY S.rating, S.sname ← both columns in GROUP BY

This is the most common GROUP BY mistake. Every non-aggregate column in SELECT must appear in GROUP BY. Adding sname to GROUP BY changes the query: groups are now (rating, sname) pairs, not just rating. COUNT(*) counts how many rows have that exact (rating, sname) combination.

◆ Ex 10 — GROUP BY with ORDER BY
SELECT S.rating, COUNT(*) AS cnt, AVG(S.age) AS avg_age FROM Sailors S GROUP BY S.rating HAVING COUNT(*) >= 1 ORDER BY avg_age DESC

Full query evaluation order: FROM → WHERE → GROUP BY → HAVING → SELECT → ORDER BY. ORDER BY can reference column aliases defined in SELECT (avg_age). Results are sorted by average age descending. ORDER BY is always the last step.

Chapter 5 · NULL Values

NULL Values — Three-Valued Logic

10 examples covering all the tricky cases

NULL and Three-Valued Logic Slide 25

NULL means "unknown" or "not applicable." SQL uses three-valued logic: TRUE, FALSE, UNKNOWN. The WHERE clause only keeps rows that evaluate to TRUE — UNKNOWN rows are discarded.

Examples
★ Slide Ex 1 — Is rating>8 true when rating is NULL?

The slide asks this directly. Answer: UNKNOWN. NULL>8 = UNKNOWN. Not TRUE, not FALSE. A row with NULL rating fails WHERE rating>8 (because UNKNOWN ≠ TRUE, so the row is excluded). This surprises beginners who expect NULL to act like 0.

◆ Ex 2 — Checking for NULL correctly
-- WRONG: never returns any rows! SELECT * FROM Sailors WHERE rating = NULL ← ALWAYS FALSE -- CORRECT: SELECT * FROM Sailors WHERE rating IS NULL SELECT * FROM Sailors WHERE rating IS NOT NULL

"rating = NULL" evaluates to UNKNOWN (not TRUE), so no row passes. "rating IS NULL" is a special predicate that correctly checks for NULL. Always use IS NULL / IS NOT NULL, never = NULL.

◆ Ex 3 — Three-valued logic truth table

Key rules: FALSE AND anything = FALSE (even FALSE AND UNKNOWN = FALSE). TRUE OR anything = TRUE (even TRUE OR UNKNOWN = TRUE). NOT UNKNOWN = UNKNOWN.

◆ Ex 4 — NULL in aggregates
-- Suppose one sailor has NULL rating: SELECT COUNT(*) FROM Sailors -- counts ALL rows including NULL-rating SELECT COUNT(rating) FROM Sailors -- counts only non-NULL ratings SELECT AVG(rating) FROM Sailors -- ignores NULL ratings in average SELECT SUM(rating) FROM Sailors -- ignores NULL ratings in sum

COUNT(*) = n. COUNT(rating) = n-1 (one NULL skipped). AVG and SUM also skip NULLs. This means AVG may not equal SUM/COUNT if there are NULLs — COUNT(*) includes NULL rows but AVG/SUM don't. Critical: if ALL values are NULL, AVG/SUM return NULL, COUNT(*) returns the count.

◆ Ex 5 — NULL propagation in arithmetic
SELECT rating + 1 FROM Sailors -- NULL+1 = NULL SELECT rating * 0 FROM Sailors -- NULL*0 = NULL (NOT 0!) SELECT COALESCE(rating, 0) + 1 -- replace NULL with 0 first

Any arithmetic with NULL produces NULL. NULL*0 is NOT 0 — it's UNKNOWN because we don't know what NULL is. COALESCE(rating, 0) replaces NULL with 0, then arithmetic works normally. COALESCE returns the first non-NULL argument.

★ Slide Ex 6 — New operators needed because of NULL (outer joins)

The slide explicitly lists "New operators (in particular, outer joins) possible/needed" as a complication of NULL. A LEFT OUTER JOIN includes all rows from the left table even if there's no matching row on the right — the right-side columns become NULL. Example: LEFT JOIN Sailors with Reserves — lubber(31) has no reservation, so bid and day are NULL for lubber's row. Without outer joins, lubber disappears from the result entirely.

Chapter 5 · Constraints & Triggers

General Constraints, ASSERTION, Triggers — Missing Details

Named CHECK Constraint — noInterlakeRes Slide 27 ★ Was missing from Guide 1

The original guide didn't reproduce this specific slide example, which shows a named CHECK constraint with a subquery.

Examples
★ Slide Ex 1 — The noInterlakeRes named constraint (exact from slides)
CREATE TABLE Reserves ( sname CHAR(10), bid INTEGER, day DATE, PRIMARY KEY (bid, day), CONSTRAINT noInterlakeRes CHECK ('Interlake' <> (SELECT B.bname FROM Boats B WHERE B.bid = bid)) )

This constraint says: "No reservation can be made for a boat named 'Interlake'." The subquery looks up the boat name for the reservation's bid. If the name equals 'Interlake', the CHECK fails and the INSERT is rejected. Naming a constraint (CONSTRAINT noInterlakeRes) lets you get a meaningful error message and DROP the constraint by name later.

★ Slide Ex 2 — ASSERTION for cross-table constraint
-- WRONG: awkward, and fails when Sailors is empty CREATE TABLE Sailors ( ... CHECK ( (SELECT COUNT(S.sid) FROM Sailors S) + (SELECT COUNT(B.bid) FROM Boats B) < 100 ) ) -- CORRECT: ASSERTION (not tied to either table) CREATE ASSERTION smallClub CHECK ( (SELECT COUNT(S.sid) FROM Sailors S) + (SELECT COUNT(B.bid) FROM Boats B) < 100 )

The slide explicitly marks the first version as "Awkward and wrong!" — if Sailors is empty, the constraint only fires when Sailors changes, not when Boats changes. ASSERTION is checked on every INSERT/UPDATE/DELETE to either table. The slides note ASSERTION is "the right solution; not associated with either table."

★ Slide Ex 3 — Trigger example (exact from slides)
CREATE TRIGGER youngSailorUpdate AFTER INSERT ON Sailors REFERENCING NEW TABLE NewSailors FOR EACH STATEMENT INSERT INTO YoungSailors (sid, name, age, rating) SELECT sid, name, age, rating FROM NewSailors N WHERE N.age <= 18

Three parts: EVENT = AFTER INSERT ON Sailors (fires after any insert). REFERENCING NEW TABLE NewSailors = the set of newly inserted rows. ACTION = INSERT qualifying young sailors into YoungSailors. FOR EACH STATEMENT = fires once per INSERT statement (not once per row). Alternative: FOR EACH ROW fires once per inserted row.

◆ Ex 4 — CHECK with simple condition vs. subquery
-- Simple domain constraint (always legal): CHECK (rating >= 1 AND rating <= 10) -- Constraint referencing another table (subquery): CHECK (bid IN (SELECT bid FROM Boats))

Simple CHECK constraints (no subquery) are always enforced. CHECK with subqueries is supported in SQL standard but not in all databases (MySQL ignores subqueries in CHECK). For cross-table constraints, use ASSERTION or triggers instead for portability.

◆ Ex 5 — BEFORE trigger (prevents operation)
CREATE TRIGGER checkRating BEFORE INSERT ON Sailors FOR EACH ROW WHEN (NEW.rating < 1 OR NEW.rating > 10) BEGIN SIGNAL SQLSTATE '45000' SET MESSAGE_TEXT = 'Invalid rating'; END

BEFORE triggers fire before the operation and can prevent it. AFTER triggers fire after (like the slide example). BEFORE is useful for validation and modification. AFTER is useful for maintaining derived tables or audit logs. The trigger in the slides uses AFTER — it doesn't prevent anything, it just keeps YoungSailors consistent.

Chapter 4B · Relational Calculus

Relational Calculus — Missing Topics

TRC vs DRC distinction · All slide DRC queries worked through

TRC vs. DRC — The Distinction Slide 2

The original guide only covered DRC. The slides distinguish two flavours:

  • Tuple Relational Calculus (TRC): Variables range over entire TUPLES. A variable t "picks up" a whole row from a relation. You access individual fields as t.sid, t.sname, etc.
  • Domain Relational Calculus (DRC): Variables range over individual FIELD VALUES (domains). Each variable binds to one column value. A query needing all 4 sailor columns needs 4 separate variables: I, N, T, A.

Both TRC and DRC are subsets of first-order predicate logic. Both have exactly the same expressive power as relational algebra (for safe queries).

All DRC queries from the slides — fully worked
★ Slide DRC Query 1 — All sailors with rating > 7
{⟨I, N, T, A⟩ | ⟨I, N, T, A⟩ ∈ Sailors ∧ T > 7}

Variables: I=sid, N=sname, T=rating (T for "type"/"tier"), A=age. ALL FOUR are FREE — they appear on the left side of |. The formula checks: (1) this tuple exists in Sailors AND (2) T > 7. Result: all sailor tuples where rating exceeds 7. From S2: yuppy(9), lubber(8), rusty(10).

The slide then asks: "Modify to find sailors older than 18 OR rating under 9, named 'Joe'." Answer: {⟨I,N,T,A⟩ | ⟨I,N,T,A⟩ ∈ Sailors ∧ N='Joe' ∧ (A>18 ∨ T<9)}

★ Slide DRC Query 2 — Sailors rated > 7 who reserved boat 103
{⟨I,N,T,A⟩ | ⟨I,N,T,A⟩ ∈ Sailors ∧ T > 7 ∧ ∃Ir,Br,D (⟨Ir,Br,D⟩ ∈ Reserves ∧ Ir=I ∧ Br=103)}

Ir, Br, D are BOUND by ∃ — they don't appear in the output. The ∃ says: "there exists a Reserves tuple with sailor-id=Ir matching our sailor I, and boat-id=103." Note: we need Ir=I (not just any reservation) — the reservation must belong to THIS specific sailor. From S1: rusty(58,10,35) has sid=58, and R1 has (58,103) — rusty qualifies. Dustin has rating=7, not >7.

★ Slide DRC Query 3 — Sailors rated > 7 who reserved a RED boat
{⟨I,N,T,A⟩ | ⟨I,N,T,A⟩ ∈ Sailors ∧ T > 7 ∧ ∃Ir,Br,D (⟨Ir,Br,D⟩ ∈ Reserves ∧ Ir=I ∧ ∃B,BN,C (⟨B,BN,C⟩ ∈ Boats ∧ B=Br ∧ C='red'))}

Now we chain two existentials: exists a reservation ∧ exists a boat that matches the reservation's bid AND is red. Note nested ∃: the inner one binds B,BN,C (boat variables) and checks colour. The slide notes: "Observe how parentheses control the scope of each quantifier's binding." Ir,Br,D,B,BN,C are all bound. I,N,T,A are free (in the output).

★ Slide DRC Query 4 — Sailors who've reserved ALL boats (with ∀)
{⟨I,N,T,A⟩ | ⟨I,N,T,A⟩ ∈ Sailors ∧ ∀B,BN,C (⟨B,BN,C⟩ ∈ Boats → ∃Ir,Br,D (⟨Ir,Br,D⟩ ∈ Reserves ∧ I=Ir ∧ Br=B))}

"For ALL 3-tuples ⟨B,BN,C⟩, IF it is a Boats tuple, THEN there EXISTS a Reserves tuple where this sailor (I) reserved that boat (B=Br)." The → (implication) is critical: ∀B,BN,C (Boats → Reserves) means "for every boat, there's a reservation." B,BN,C,Ir,Br,D all bound by ∀ or ∃.

The slides also show a simplified notation using ∀ with just B,BN,C as a shorthand for the triple ∀ quantifier.

★ Slide DRC Query 5 — Sailors who've reserved all RED boats
{⟨I,N,T,A⟩ | ⟨I,N,T,A⟩ ∈ Sailors ∧ ∀B,BN,C ((⟨B,BN,C⟩ ∈ Boats ∧ C='red') → ∃Ir,Br,D (⟨Ir,Br,D⟩ ∈ Reserves ∧ I=Ir ∧ Br=B))}

Same as above but the antecedent of → now has an extra condition: C='red'. "For every tuple ⟨B,BN,C⟩ that is BOTH a Boats tuple AND is red, there's a reservation by sailor I." Boats that are NOT red are vacuously satisfied (the implication is trivially true when C≠'red').

★ Slide — Unsafe query example
{S | ¬(⟨S⟩ ∈ Sailors)}

This says: "all tuples S such that S is NOT in Sailors." This is UNSAFE — there are infinitely many tuples not in Sailors. The answer is undefined/infinite. Safe queries restrict answers to values that appear in the database's active domain (values actually occurring in some relation). All exam-relevant queries are safe.

◆ Practice — Convert RA to DRC: π_sname(σ_rating=10(Sailors))
{⟨N⟩ | ∃I,T,A (⟨I,N,T,A⟩ ∈ Sailors ∧ T = 10)}

Only N is free (we want sname). I,T,A are bound by ∃. The formula: there exists a complete Sailors tuple with this N and T=10. This is the DRC translation of the RA expression. Both return the set of names of sailors with rating 10.
